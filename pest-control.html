Detailed guide on crawling insects, flying insects, rodents, and more.

Crawling Insects

Ants

Ants belong to the Formicidae family and are among the most hardworking insects in the world. Living in large colonies, they are considered eusocial insects, with a highly organized structure. Colonies consist of a queen (responsible for reproduction), workers (infertile females handling food, care, and defense), and drones (males that mate with the queen).

Life Cycle of Ants
  • • Egg: Laid by the queen, usually in underground nests or crevices.
  • • Larva: Fed and protected by worker ants.
  • • Pupa: Undergo metamorphosis into adult form.
  • • Adult: Fully developed ant, taking up roles in the colony.
Common Types of Ants
  • • Fire Ants: Reddish-brown, known for painful stings that may trigger allergies.
  • • Carpenter Ants: Large black ants that damage wooden structures.
  • • Pharaoh Ants: Small yellow ants often found in homes and hospitals.
  • • Odorous Ants: Release a strong smell when crushed, attracted to sweets.
How They Harm Us
  • • Contaminate food sources while foraging.
  • • Spread bacteria and pathogens.
  • • Damage wooden structures and property (Carpenter ants).
  • • Painful bites or stings (Fire ants, Harvester ants).

Termites

Termites, often called “silent destroyers”, feed primarily on cellulose found in wood, paper, and plants. They live in highly organized colonies with a caste system that includes workers, soldiers, and reproductives (kings & queens). Despite their small size, they can cause massive structural damage if left untreated.

Life Cycle of Termites
  • • Egg: Queens lay thousands of tiny eggs in hidden nests.
  • • Nymph: Developing termites that can grow into different castes (workers, soldiers, reproductives).
  • • Adult: Fully developed termites take on specialized roles within the colony.
Types of Termites
  • • Subterranean Termites: Build mud tubes and attack foundations, causing severe damage.
  • • Drywood Termites: Infest dry wood and furniture without needing soil contact.
  • • Dampwood Termites: Thrive in moist or decaying wood.
How They Harm Us
  • • Destroy wooden structures, flooring, and furniture.
  • • Weaken building foundations, posing safety risks.
  • • Damage paper, books, and fabrics containing cellulose.
  • • Cost billions globally in structural repairs each year.

Bed Bugs

Bed bugs are small, reddish-brown, wingless insects that feed exclusively on the blood of humans and animals. They are nocturnal and hide in cracks, mattresses, bed frames, and furniture during the day, making them very difficult to detect. Despite not spreading diseases, their bites cause irritation, stress, and allergic reactions.

Life Cycle of Bed Bugs
  • • Egg: Tiny, white, and laid in clusters in hidden areas.
  • • Nymph: Passes through 5 molts, needing a blood meal each time.
  • • Adult: Fully developed, can survive months without feeding.
Where They Hide
  • • Mattresses, bed frames, and headboards.
  • • Cracks in walls, furniture, and skirting boards.
  • • Behind wallpapers, picture frames, and electrical sockets.
How They Harm Us
  • • Cause itchy, red bite marks, often in clusters or lines.
  • • Lead to sleeplessness, stress, and anxiety.
  • • Can trigger allergic reactions in sensitive individuals.
  • • Spread quickly through luggage, clothing, and second-hand furniture.

Cockroaches

Cockroaches are among the most resilient pests on earth, known for their fast reproduction, adaptability, and ability to thrive in unsanitary conditions. Common species include the German, American, and Oriental cockroaches. They are nocturnal and often hide in warm, dark, and damp areas of homes and businesses.

Life Cycle of Cockroaches
  • • Egg (ootheca): Egg cases contain multiple eggs and are often hidden in crevices.
  • • Nymph: Resembles a smaller adult, molts several times before maturing.
  • • Adult: Fully developed, capable of reproducing rapidly.
Where They Hide
  • • Kitchens, bathrooms, and drains.
  • • Behind refrigerators, stoves, and sinks.
  • • Inside cracks, cupboards, and electrical appliances.
How They Harm Us
  • • Contaminate food and utensils with bacteria, viruses, and parasites.
  • • Spread diseases like salmonella, dysentery, and gastroenteritis.
  • • Trigger asthma and allergies through their droppings, shed skins, and saliva.
  • • Unpleasant odor that lingers in infested areas.

Fleas

Fleas are tiny, wingless, blood-sucking parasites that primarily infest pets like cats and dogs but can also bite humans. Their powerful hind legs allow them to jump long distances compared to their size, making them difficult to catch and control.

Life Cycle of Fleas
  • • Egg: Laid on pets or in their bedding, quickly falls into the environment.
  • • Larva: Feeds on organic matter, including adult flea feces.
  • • Pupa: Protective cocoon stage, can stay dormant for months.
  • • Adult: Seeks a host for blood meals to survive and reproduce.
Where They Hide
  • • Pet fur, carpets, sofas, and pet bedding.
  • • Cracks in flooring or furniture.
  • • Outdoors in shady, moist areas like grass and soil.
How They Harm Us
  • • Cause itchy bites leading to skin irritation and allergic reactions.
  • • Transmit tapeworms and diseases such as typhus and plague.
  • • Severe infestations may lead to anemia in pets due to blood loss.

Spiders

Spiders are arachnids, not insects, and are known for their eight legs and ability to produce silk. While most spiders are harmless and help by preying on pests like flies and mosquitoes, some species can be dangerous due to their venomous bites.

Life Cycle of Spiders
  • • Egg: Laid in silk sacs, protected until hatching.
  • • Spiderlings: Hatchlings that disperse by “ballooning” on silk threads.
  • • Adult: Fully developed with the ability to spin webs, hunt, and reproduce.
Where They Hide
  • • Corners of ceilings, basements, and attics.
  • • Dark, undisturbed areas like behind furniture.
  • • Outdoor spaces like gardens, wood piles, and sheds.
How They Harm Us
  • • Some species (Black Widow, Brown Recluse) have venomous bites causing serious health issues.
  • • Webs create an untidy and unhygienic appearance in homes.
  • • Fear of spiders (arachnophobia) is common and causes discomfort for many people.

Silverfish

Silverfish are small, wingless insects with a shiny, silver-gray body that moves in a fish-like manner. They are nocturnal and thrive in dark, damp environments such as basements, bathrooms, and kitchens.

Life Cycle
  • • Egg: Tiny, white eggs hidden in cracks.
  • • Nymph: Young silverfish look like smaller adults.
  • • Adult: Fully grown in about 3–4 months.
How They Harm Us
  • • Damage to books, wallpaper, and important papers.
  • • Contaminate food items containing starch and sugar.
  • • Cause gradual structural damage if infestations spread.

Woodworm

Woodworm is not a single pest, but a term for the larvae of various wood-boring beetles. These larvae tunnel through timber, leaving behind holes and a powdery residue known as frass.

Life Cycle
  • • Egg: Laid in cracks of wooden surfaces.
  • • Larva: Feed inside timber, causing the most damage.
  • • Pupa & Adult: Emerge through holes, continuing the cycle.
How They Harm Us
  • • Weakening of furniture and structural wood.
  • • Visible exit holes and crumbling wood surfaces.
  • • Long-term infestations can lead to unsafe structures.

Textile Pests

Textile pests include insects such as clothes moths and carpet beetles that feed on natural fibers like wool, silk, and cotton. They thrive in wardrobes, carpets, and upholstered furniture.

Life Cycle
  • • Egg: Laid on fabrics and fibers.
  • • Larva: Feed on fibers, causing holes and thinning.
  • • Adult: Mainly for reproduction, not feeding.
How They Harm Us
  • • Damage to clothes, carpets, and upholstery.
  • • Financial loss due to replacement of damaged items.
  • • Infestation spreads quickly if left untreated.

Flying Insects

Flies

Flies are one of the most common and bothersome flying pests found worldwide. They breed quickly in warm, moist, and unhygienic environments such as garbage, animal waste, and stagnant water. Their short life cycle allows populations to multiply rapidly, making infestations difficult to control once established. They constantly move between waste and food sources, making them highly effective disease carriers.

How They Harm Us
  • • Transmit diseases like cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and food poisoning.
  • • Contaminate food and surfaces with bacteria by regurgitation and fecal matter.
  • • Spread intestinal parasites and harmful microorganisms to humans and pets.
  • • Cause serious outbreaks of food-borne illness in kitchens, restaurants, and markets.

Moths

Moths are nocturnal flying insects closely related to butterflies. While adult moths are mostly harmless and feed on nectar, their larvae (caterpillars) can cause significant damage. Clothes moth larvae feed on natural fibers like wool, silk, and cotton, while pantry moths infest stored food products such as rice, cereals, flour, and nuts. Because they prefer dark, undisturbed areas like closets, attics, and storage rooms, moth infestations often go unnoticed until damage becomes severe.

How They Harm Us
  • • Clothes moth larvae destroy garments, carpets, and upholstery made from natural fibers.
  • • Pantry moths contaminate food supplies, making grains and cereals inedible.
  • • Larvae infestations spread quickly due to hidden breeding spots.
  • • Costly damage to textiles, stored food, and household belongings.

Mosquitoes & Midges

Mosquitoes and midges are small but highly irritating flying insects. Mosquitoes breed in stagnant or slow-moving water and are active mainly at dawn and dusk. Only female mosquitoes bite, feeding on blood to develop eggs. Midges, though smaller, can swarm in large numbers, especially near water bodies, and inflict painful bites. Together, these pests are not only a nuisance but also among the most dangerous insects to human health.

How They Harm Us
  • • Mosquitoes spread malaria, dengue, chikungunya, Zika virus, and filariasis.
  • • Bites cause intense itching, skin irritation, and allergic reactions.
  • • Midges swarm in outdoor spaces, disrupting activities and causing painful welts.
  • • High populations in residential areas reduce comfort and quality of life.

Wasps

Wasps are aggressive flying insects that often nest in sheltered spots like roof eaves, wall cavities, trees, and shrubs. Unlike bees, wasps can sting multiple times without losing their stinger, making them more dangerous in swarms. They feed on sugary substances and insects, and colonies grow larger in warmer months. Disturbing a wasp nest can provoke an immediate and aggressive response.

How They Harm Us
  • • Painful stings that cause redness, swelling, and intense irritation.
  • • Multiple stings can be dangerous, especially to children and pets.
  • • Can trigger severe allergic reactions and anaphylaxis in sensitive individuals.
  • • Aggressive behavior near nests makes outdoor areas unsafe.

Rodents & Others

Rodents

Rodents such as rats and mice are among the most common and destructive pests worldwide. They breed quickly, chew through wood, wires, and packaging, and contaminate food supplies with droppings and urine. Their gnawing behavior causes extensive damage to property and increases the risk of electrical fires.

How They Harm Us
  • • Spread serious diseases like leptospirosis, salmonella, and plague.
  • • Contaminate food and water with urine, feces, and fur.
  • • Gnaw on electrical wires, increasing fire hazards.
  • • Cause structural damage to homes, warehouses, and storage facilities.

Birds

While birds may not always be seen as pests, certain species such as pigeons, crows, and seagulls can create significant problems in urban and industrial areas. Their droppings are acidic, corroding building materials and vehicles, while their nests block ventilation systems and gutters.

How They Harm Us
  • • Spread diseases including histoplasmosis, salmonella, and cryptococcosis.
  • • Bird droppings damage buildings, vehicles, and statues.
  • • Nests block gutters, drains, and HVAC systems, causing flooding or malfunctions.
  • • Aggressive behavior during nesting season poses safety risks.

Stored Product Insects

Stored product insects include beetles, weevils, and moths that infest dry goods such as grains, cereals, flour, and nuts. These pests thrive in warehouses, grocery stores, and homes, often spreading unnoticed until food products are heavily contaminated.

How They Harm Us
  • • Contaminate large quantities of food with larvae, eggs, and webbing.
  • • Cause significant economic loss in storage and retail industries.
  • • Reduce the quality, taste, and safety of stored food products.
  • • Infestations spread rapidly due to hidden breeding in packaging.

Snakes

Snakes are not insects but are often considered pests when they invade human habitats. While many snake species are non-venomous and help control rodent populations, venomous snakes pose serious risks to people and pets when they enter gardens, farms, or buildings.

How They Harm Us
  • • Venomous bites can be life-threatening and require immediate medical attention.
  • • Fear and panic caused by snake sightings disrupt normal life.
  • • Presence in agricultural fields endangers workers and livestock.
  • • Can enter homes and storage areas in search of food (rodents).
